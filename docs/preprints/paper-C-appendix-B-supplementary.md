**Companion supplementary to** *Articulation Skill: A Patient-Side Literacy Regime for Public-Good Mental-Health AI* (Wu 2026, Zenodo DOI assigned on upload). The main paper's §1.4 names the five literatures in summary; this file does the full disambiguation.

## Appendix B · Lineage disambiguation (full)

§1.4 names five literatures the regime engages. The disambiguation pattern is the same in each section: name the borrowed concept, identify the structural difference that prevents the regime from being read as a sub-type, and mark the predictable cost the borrowed tradition's own critics would name. The map across sections is: B.1 (Strauss/Star) sets the patient-side-capability framing the rest depends on; B.2 (Mishler/Kleinman) is the inverse-direction case where the regime moves *toward* the institutional voice the tradition critiqued; B.3 (Nutbeam/Hibbard) is the measurement-tradition kin; B.4 (Elwyn/decision-aids) is the closest sibling, distinguished by front-door context (B.4 (c)); B.5 (CMS PIN) is function-class kinship not billing-pathway candidacy; B.6 (AI-safety) partitions citations by deployment-layer engagement vs training-layer residual.

### B.1 Strauss articulation-work, Star category-work, and the artifact's genre

Strauss (1985, 1988, 1993) defined "articulation work" as cooperative coordination labor across multiple actors with different roles — the invisible work that meshes fragmented care delivery. Schmidt and Bannon (1992, *CSCW: An International Journal* vol 1) extended this with the claim that articulation work is required precisely because complete standardization is impossible. Our "articulation skill" names a unilateral patient-side capability of translating undifferentiated experience into a bounded institutional vocabulary; this is closer to Star (1991) on category-work and the standardization-of-conventions cost — the labor of matching one's experience to institutional categories not built to fit (Star, "Power, technology and the phenomenology of conventions: On being allergic to onions," in J. Law (ed.), *A Sociology of Monsters*, Routledge; and Star (1991), "The sociology of the invisible: The primacy of work in the writings of Anselm Strauss," in D. Maines (ed.), *Social Organization and Social Process*, Aldine de Gruyter) — than to Strauss articulation work in the cooperative CSCW sense. The term "articulation skill" is repurposed: it names the patient-side capability of "finding the words for it," not the cooperative-coordination labor across distributed actors that Strauss and Schmidt-Bannon defined. Strauss articulation work is constitutively cooperative; the regime's articulation skill is constitutively unilateral (one user, one AI, no role-coordination). A reader expecting the cooperative meaning will find the regime does not deliver it. The repurposed term is closer to Star's (1991) category-work in operational shape; we use "articulation" because that is the colloquial English term for what users do when they translate experience into clinical-adjacent vocabulary, and the colloquial use is what the regime directly addresses. The term invokes a Strauss/Schmidt-Bannon tradition whose canonical move (filling gaps that standardization cannot reach) is in tension with the §3.3 design choice (binding vocabulary to institutional standards). The §3.3 inclusion criterion places the regime on the *standardization* side of the Schmidt-Bannon distinction, and the §7.4 risk-population list is the predictable Star-tradition output of that choice, not a list of items to fix one at a time. The two traditions predict different things about the regime: Schmidt-Bannon predicts that cooperative articulation work will still be required downstream (clinician and patient renegotiating what the artifact misses), and the regime accepts this — the artifact is preparation for that conversation, not a substitute for it. Star predicts that the standardization itself imposes costs on those who do not fit the categories, and §7.4 is that cost made explicit. The Star tradition does not accept utilitarian cost-benefit framing as a methodological response either, parallel to B.2's treatment of Mishler/Kleinman; the regime sits inside the harm class Star's tradition critiques, and we do not pretend that crediting Star with predictive accuracy on §7.4 discharges the normative force of Star's position on §3.3. Bounded vocabulary is what makes the patient-portable artifact deployable at any door, and that is the choice made; it is not a choice Star's tradition would endorse.

A predictable CSCW objection to the term itself. A reviewer in the CSCW tradition will read the choice to call this regime "articulation skill" — given that "articulation work" is a term of art with 40+ years of cooperative-coordination meaning — as disciplinary appropriation, and the "colloquial English" defense above as inadequate. The strongest version of the objection: a unilateral standardization-binding capability should not be named with a CSCW term whose canonical meaning is the inverse on both axes (cooperation vs unilateral, gap-filling vs standardization-binding). We engage the objection rather than route around it. Three reasons we keep the term despite the objection. (i) "Articulation" has substantial colloquial use in clinical and intake settings predating and parallel to the CSCW term-of-art ("the patient articulated their concern as…", "help the patient articulate what they are experiencing"). The colloquial usage is the source the regime addresses; the CSCW lineage is named to disambiguate, not to claim. (ii) The regime *does* sit in continuity with Strauss-Schmidt-Bannon at the level of the ecosystem the artifact participates in: Schmidt-Bannon's prediction (cooperative articulation work is still required downstream of any artifact) is one of the regime's load-bearing acceptances — the artifact is preparation for that downstream work, not a substitute. The terminological link signals this continuity even where the design choice on the standardization axis inverts. (iii) Renaming has its own costs: the alternatives we considered (patient-side translation skill, intake preparation skill, navigation literacy) each lose a different piece of what the regime does, and a name is judged against the alternatives that exist, not against an ideal name that would not invoke any tradition. We accept that this is a contested terminological choice, and acknowledge that a reader who weighs the appropriation concern more heavily than the colloquial-usage and continuity-with-downstream-work arguments will reasonably prefer one of the alternatives.

The §3.1 patient-portable literacy artifact is, as noted above, a *delegated artifact* or portable referral letter rather than a boundary object. The CSCW genre literature on coordinative paper-form artifacts (Berg & Bowker 1997 *The Sociological Quarterly* on the medical record as a coordinative artifact; Sellen & Harper 2002 *The Myth of the Paperless Office*) predicts specific failure modes for artifacts in this genre — selective transcription, format-driven omission, the tendency of "what the next reader needs" to crowd out "what the patient said," and reciprocal pressure on the next reader to treat the artifact as authoritative rather than provisional. The §3.1 artifact is structurally exposed to these; §10.3's articulation-quality study is where the regime's actual exposure to them would be empirically tested. The genre literature is sharper than the boundary-object frame and is the tradition the §10.3 measurement work should engage in revision.

The §3.1 literacy artifact is not a boundary object. A boundary object (Star & Griesemer 1989; Star & Bowker 1999) requires plasticity to local needs of several parties, robustness across translations, and sitting *between* communities of practice. The §3.1 artifact is produced by one community (AI + user) and read by another (downstream clinician); it is a *delegated artifact* or portable referral letter.

### B.2 Mishler voice-of-medicine and Kleinman explanatory models

Mishler (1984, *The Discourse of Medicine*) argues that the clinical encounter is a power-asymmetric translation in which voice-of-medicine *suppresses* voice-of-lifeworld. Kleinman (1978, 1980, 1988 *The Illness Narratives*, 1995 *Writing at the Margin*) argues that patient explanatory models have clinical and ethical weight that biomedical translation strips, and that the *clinician* is supposed to negotiate the gap, not standardize toward the biomedical. The articulation regime performs upstream the standardization Mishler critiques and Kleinman asks the clinician to resist: an AI that translates the patient's lifeworld content into modality and credential vocabulary before the clinician sees the patient is doing voice-of-medicine suppression with extra steps. Mishler's voice-of-medicine is moved earlier in the clinical pathway under this regime; that is the move, and naming it as such matters more than calling it an inversion. The §3.3 vocabulary-bounding is the explanatory-model standardization Kleinman's tradition resists.

The regime concedes the structural critique: this is voice-of-medicine suppression performed upstream of the clinical encounter, eliminating the site where Kleinman asks the clinician to negotiate the patient's explanatory model against the biomedical. The Mishler/Kleinman tradition does not accept utilitarian cost-benefit framing as a methodological response, and we do not offer one. The regime sits inside the harm class their tradition critiques. This is the inverse direction from B.1: there the regime accepts a *Star-tradition* cost (standardization disadvantages those who do not fit categories) as the price of portability; here the regime moves *toward* the institutional voice that Mishler and Kleinman identified as the locus of harm, and the cost is correspondingly heavier — a fact the harm-reduction comparator below addresses but does not erase.

What the regime offers instead is *harm-reduction relative to a counterfactual*. The relevant comparator is not the clinical encounter Mishler diagnosed but the current state for the in-scope subset (§7.5.4): users routing mental-health-relevant conversation to ChatGPT/Claude/general-purpose-LLMs, where (a) the same lifeworld-to-vocabulary translation occurs without explicit lineage acknowledgment, (b) the negotiation Kleinman calls for is *equally* foreclosed, (c) the failure modes documented in §4.8(i)–(iv) (sycophancy, confabulation, no-safety-section-retention-on-fork, AI-induced psychosis) are unbounded by any stated regime. The regime's claim is that *given the counterfactual*, a deployment that performs the suppression *with named lineage tensions, bounded vocabulary, scope-recognition constraint, and articulation-quality measurement as a research program (§10.3)* is harm-reductive in expectation, not harm-eliminative. A reader who treats the comparator as the well-resourced clinical encounter Mishler diagnosed will judge the regime as net-harm; a reader who treats the comparator as no-fitted-destination plus general-LLM venue will judge it as net-helpful. The in-tradition response to the first reader is deferred to §10.3, where articulation-quality measurement against downstream-clinician outcomes is the empirical pathway by which the harm-reduction claim can be tested against the tradition's standards. §7.4 names the cost.

### B.3 Nutbeam health literacy and Hibbard PAM

Nutbeam (2000, *Health Promotion International*) defined a three-tier health-literacy framework (functional / interactive / critical). The §3.3 vocabulary tiers operate in the interactive-literacy band; whether the regime *builds* critical-literacy capacity or *substitutes* for it is an open empirical question we do not resolve. Hibbard et al. (2004, *Health Services Research*) developed the Patient Activation Measure (PAM) as a longitudinal trait-level instrument; PAM stages 1–4 are stages of multi-month change, not session-level states. Within-session articulation outcomes are not directly comparable to PAM stage transitions; the relationship is at most that PAM trait change might be triangulated against repeated articulation-session outcomes over months, a longitudinal study the regime has not specified. The PAM measurement tradition and the decision-aids measurement tradition (B.4: O'Connor 1995 DCS, IPDAS) are the two closest measurement-side neighbors; §10.3's articulation-quality construct is the work neither has standing instruments for.

### B.4 Elwyn shared-decision-making and patient-decision-aids — kin, not sub-type

Elwyn et al.'s shared-decision-making framework (2012) operates inside the clinical encounter; Elwyn's later work and the patient-decision-aids tradition (Stacey et al. 2024 Cochrane review CD001431, sixth update; the IPDAS Collaboration; validated instruments including the Decisional Conflict Scale (O'Connor 1995), Decision Self-Efficacy Scale, SURE test, and Preparation for Decision Making scale) sit upstream of SDM, in the same slot the articulation regime occupies. Three structural differences hold the regime claim against the decision-aid-sub-type reading:

- **(a)** The decision-aids tradition presupposes a treatment decision (Elwyn 2012; the IPDAS defining criterion is preparation-for-decision). Articulation skill operates upstream of any presupposed decision and supports users who do not yet know what kind of decision they face.
- **(b)** The decision-aids tradition is structured around evidence-based options for a defined clinical condition (CD001431 includes decision aids for screening, surgery, treatment selection given a known diagnosis). Articulation skill operates without a known diagnosis and refuses to render one (Property 3 tier b).
- **(c)** The decision-aids tradition assumes the user has already entered the clinical pipeline that selects them in (referrals, intake screens, clinician judgment that a decision is approaching); the regime operates in an *unfiltered front-door context* where no upstream pipeline has performed that selection. This requires the regime to take on a filtering layer (the §3.4 scope-recognition constraint) the decision-aids tradition does not need. The structural difference is the front-door-vs-filtered context, not "the regime has §3.4."

These are structural differences sufficient to position the regime as adjacent to and upstream of decision aids, not a sub-type within them. Kinship and distinction both hold. The §4 prior-art search will be extended in revision to inspect Cochrane CD001431's included studies and the IPDAS inventory; results in §A.2.

### B.5 CMS Principal Illness Navigation — function-class kin, not billing-pathway candidate

CMS established Principal Illness Navigation billing codes (G0023, G0024) effective 2024-01-01, formalizing the patient-navigator role for serious-illness patients (cancer first, extensible to others). CMS PIN reimburses *human* labor performed by a trained team member with documented competencies, with documented warm handoff and confirmed follow-through. The articulation regime as deployed has none of these (no clinician supervision relationship, no documented competency framework, no team integration, no warm handoff with confirmed contact). The §6.1 handoff metric is documented within-session intent only, not documented follow-through. The lineage citation here is *function-class kin, not billing-pathway candidate*: the regime occupies the same agency-function neighborhood as PIN-reimbursed human navigators (translating clinical complexity, preparing patients for handoff), but it is not on a near-term reimbursement trajectory and is not positioned as one. The relationship to B.4 is parallel: there the regime is *upstream of* the decision-aids slot (decision is not yet identified); here the regime is *upstream of* the PIN slot (the patient has not yet entered a navigator-eligible care relationship). The September 2025 *Journal of Oncology Navigation & Survivorship* AI-navigators issue and Montefiore Einstein's MyEleanor (33% rescheduling among 2,400 nonadherent colonoscopy patients, ASCO 2024) are adjacent AI-navigator deployments in oncology; the articulation regime is the mental-health-AI sibling, with three additional operational constraints (no provider recommendation, system-agnostic patient-owned output, vocabulary scope deliberately bounded). The contribution is the application and operationalization in the mental-health AI context.

### B.6 AI safety / specification-gaming canon

The §6.2 gaming-mode analysis is *adversarial reward-shaping* analysis (proposing a metric and asking which games it enables). The borrowing carries the safety community's predictions about which gaming patterns are addressable at the deployment layer (system-prompt level, against a frozen base model) versus at the training layer (RLHF preference signals, base-model behavior). The regime's defenses are at the deployment layer only; training-layer references are cited to characterize residual risk the deployment layer cannot fully neutralize.

*Deployment-layer relevant* (the regime's defenses bite here): Krakovna et al. 2020 (specification gaming, DeepMind blog and curated-examples list) supplies the gaming-mode taxonomy §6.2 enumerates against; Goodhart 1975 with Manheim & Garrabrant 2018 (Goodhart variants, arXiv:1803.04585) supplies the metric-pressure framing the rubric-as-metric is exposed to; Perez et al. 2022 (model-written evaluations, arXiv:2212.09251) supplies the auto-generated red-teaming methodology that is the candidate operationalization for the "stratified red-teaming of the rubric" defense named in §6.2 against §6.3's recursive-Goodhart concern.

*Training-layer residual* (the regime's defenses do not reach these; cited as bounds on what deployment-layer work can claim): Hubinger et al. 2019 (mesa-optimization, arXiv:1906.01820) and Shah et al. 2022 (goal misgeneralization, arXiv:2210.01790) describe failure modes of the base model that deployment-layer prompting cannot detect or correct; Sharma et al. 2023 (sycophancy, arXiv:2310.13548) and Casper et al. 2023 (RLHF problems, arXiv:2307.15217) describe RLHF-trained dispositions that deployment-layer instruction may dampen but does not remove. Their presence in the citation set is to mark the residual the regime carries from the base model into deployment, not to claim the regime addresses them. The Hubinger 2019 citation deserves a sharper treatment: mesa-optimization is a property of *the base model the deployment runs on*, not only of a deployer's training pipeline. A frozen base model may already carry mesa-objectives developed during its own training that are misaligned with articulation quality on the distress distribution; the deployment-layer system prompt does not detect, measure, or correct these. The §6.2 audit's appropriateness rubric is the only deployment-side instrument with even partial bite on this concern (it can detect behavioral signatures at the conversation-arc level), and the rubric is not built specifically to detect mesa-objective signatures. We name this as a residual the deployment substrate carries, not as a problem the regime addresses; a mesa-objective-aware audit dimension is a candidate §10.4-class extension, not a current capability.
